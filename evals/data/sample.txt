Retrieval-augmented generation grounds a language model in an external
knowledge base so that answers can cite real source material instead of relying
solely on parametric memory. A typical pipeline ingests documents, splits them
into retrievable chunks, embeds each chunk into a vector, and stores those
vectors for similarity search at query time.

Chunking sits at the center of that pipeline. The way a document is divided
determines what the retriever can find and how much irrelevant context it drags
along. Chunks that are too large dilute the signal and waste the context window;
chunks that are too small fracture ideas across boundaries and lose the
surrounding meaning a reader needs to make sense of a passage.

Fixed-size chunking is the simplest strategy. It ignores sentence, paragraph,
and section boundaries entirely and slices the text stream into windows of a set
length. Its virtue is predictability: every chunk except the last has exactly the
same size, throughput is trivial to reason about, and there are no surprises in
how the document is partitioned. Its weakness is that it cuts through the middle
of sentences and ideas, which can strand an important clause at the edge of a
chunk where the retriever is less likely to surface it intact.

Because it is so simple, fixed-size chunking makes an honest baseline. Structure
aware strategies, recursive splitters, and semantic or model-driven approaches
all promise better boundaries, but that promise only means something when it is
measured against a reference. This evaluation records the concrete behavior of
the fixed-size strategy across several window sizes so later strategies have real
numbers to beat rather than intuitions to argue with.
